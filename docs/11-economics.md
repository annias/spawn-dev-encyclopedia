# GAME DEVELOPMENT LANGUAGE & CONCEPTS ENCYCLOPEDIA

## Chapter 11 — Studio Structure & Industry Economics

---

# The Core Mental Shift: What “The Industry” Actually Is

Beginners often think the game industry is:

> “People making games.”

Professionally, it is:

> A multi-layered economic ecosystem of studios, publishers, investors, platform holders, outsourcing networks, and middleware providers coordinating risk, capital, production capacity, and market distribution.

A key AAA truth:

> “Most game development decisions are not creative decisions — they are risk and capital allocation decisions.”

---

# Chapter 1: The Industry Ecosystem Map

At a high level, the industry is composed of:

```
Platform Holders    ↓Publishers    ↓Studios    ↓Co-Development / Outsourcing    ↓Middleware / Tooling Providers
```

Each layer has different incentives.

---

# Chapter 2: Studio Types

---

## Indie Studio

Definition:

> Small team, independent funding or self-funded development.

Characteristics:

- High creative freedom
- Limited budget
- High risk tolerance
- Fast iteration

Common tradeoff:

> Freedom vs stability

---

## AA Studio

Definition:

> Mid-sized studio between indie and AAA.

Characteristics:

- Moderate budgets
- Publisher relationships
- Hybrid creative/production structure

---

## AAA Studio

Definition:

> Large-scale production teams backed by major publishers.

Characteristics:

- Hundreds to thousands of developers
- High production value
- Strong pipeline structure
- Heavy specialization

Tradeoff:

> Scale vs agility

---

## First-Party Studio

Owned by platform holder.

Examples:

- Sony Interactive Entertainment studios
- Xbox Game Studios

Advantages:

- Platform support
- Funding stability

---

## Third-Party Studio

Independent or publisher-funded studio.

---

## Live Service Studio

Focused on continuously operating games.

Characteristics:

- Data-driven development
- Seasonal content pipelines
- Long-term monetization focus

---

# Chapter 3: Publishers (Critical Industry Layer)

---

## Publisher

Entity responsible for:

- Funding development
- Marketing
- Distribution
- Platform relationships
- Risk absorption

---

## Publishing Agreement

Contract between studio and publisher defining:

- Budget
- Revenue share
- Milestones
- IP ownership

---

## IP (Intellectual Property)

Ownership of game world/brand.

Example:

- Who owns the franchise?

---

## Royalty

Percentage of revenue paid to studio or publisher.

---

## Recoupment

Publisher recovers investment before profit sharing begins.

---

# Chapter 4: Funding Models

---

## Self-Funded

Studio finances development independently.

Risk:

- High financial exposure

---

## Publisher-Funded

Publisher finances production.

Tradeoff:

- Less creative control

---

## Venture Capital (VC)

Investment from funds expecting growth and return.

Common in:

- Mobile
- Live service startups

---

## Early Access Funding

Players fund development via early access purchases.

---

# Chapter 5: Outsourcing & Co-Development

---

## Outsourcing

External teams produce specific assets or systems.

Examples:

- Art production
- Animation
- QA testing

---

## Co-Development

External studio contributes directly to core game development.

More integrated than outsourcing.

---

## Vendor

External service provider.

Examples:

- Motion capture studios
- Audio studios

---

## Pipeline Fragmentation

When work is split across multiple companies.

Risk:

- Communication loss
- Inconsistent quality

---

# Chapter 6: Middleware & Tooling Ecosystem

---

## Middleware

External software integrated into game engine.

Examples:

- Physics engines
- Audio systems (FMOD, Wwise)
- Networking libraries

---

## Engine Licensing

Using third-party engine:

- Unreal Engine
- Unity

Tradeoff:

- Speed vs control

---

## Proprietary Engine

Studio-built engine.

Advantages:

- Full control
- Optimization freedom

Disadvantages:

- High maintenance cost

---

# Chapter 7: Production Capital Flow

---

## Budget

Total funding allocated to project.

---

## Burn Rate

How quickly money is spent monthly.

---

## Headcount Scaling

Increasing team size over time.

Important insight:

> More people does not always mean faster development.

---

## Productivity Curve

Non-linear relationship between team size and output.

Large teams introduce:

- Communication overhead
- Coordination complexity

---

# Chapter 8: Risk Economics in Game Development

---

## Development Risk

Game may not be fun or viable.

---

## Technical Risk

Engine or systems may not support design goals.

---

## Market Risk

Game may not succeed commercially.

---

## Production Risk

Game may not ship on time.

---

## Risk Mitigation Strategy

Examples:

- Prototyping
- Vertical slices
- Scope reduction
- Parallel development

---

# Chapter 14: Industry Reality Principles

---

## Principle 1: Creativity is constrained by capital

More funding → more scope  
But also more oversight

---

## Principle 2: Time is the most expensive resource

Delays cost more than features.

---

## Principle 3: Communication overhead scales non-linearly

More people = more complexity.

---

## Principle 4: Risk is always being redistributed

Every decision shifts risk somewhere:

- To production
- To technology
- To market

---

# Chapter 15: Senior Industry Mental Model

---

## Beginner Thinking

> “What game should we make?”

---

## Intermediate Thinking

> “How do we fund and build this game?”

---

## Senior Thinking

> “How do we structure creative, technical, and financial risk so that the project remains viable from concept to launch?”

---

## Expert Thinking

> “How do we align incentives across developers, publishers, platforms, and players so that creative output, financial sustainability, and production reality converge into a shippable system?”

---

# Key Insight

In professional game development:

> Studios do not simply make games — they operate within a network of financial, technical, and organizational constraints that shape what games are even possible.

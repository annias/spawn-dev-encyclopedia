# The Core Mental Shift: What “Live Service” Actually Means

Live service is often misunderstood as:

> “Updating the game after launch.”

In professional terms, it is:

> A continuously operated product system where gameplay, content, and economy are iteratively adjusted based on player behavior data, retention modeling, and business performance signals.

A key AAA framing:

> “A live game is never finished — it is continuously optimized for engagement, retention, and long-term value.”

This introduces a major shift:

- From **static design**
- To **adaptive systems driven by data**

---

# Chapter 1: The Live Service Loop

---

## The Core Loop of Live Service Games

```
Acquire Players → Engage Players → Measure Behavior → Adjust Systems → Retain Players → Repeat
```

Every live service game operates on this cycle.

---

## Acquisition

Getting players into the game.

Sources:

- Marketing
- Social media
- Platform featuring
- Influencers

---

## Engagement

Keeping players active.

Mechanisms:

- Daily rewards
- Events
- Progression systems
- Social systems

---

## Retention

Ensuring players return over time.

Measured across:

- Days
- Weeks
- Months

---

## Monetization

Converting engagement into revenue.

---

# Chapter 2: Analytics Language (Core Industry Vocabulary)

---

## Analytics

The measurement of player behavior through data.

---

## DAU (Daily Active Users)

Number of unique players per day.

---

## MAU (Monthly Active Users)

Unique players per month.

---

## DAU/MAU Ratio

Measures engagement intensity.

Higher ratio = more daily engagement.

---

## Retention Rate

Percentage of players returning after a period.

Common measures:

- D1 retention (next day)
- D7 retention
- D30 retention

---

## Churn

Players leaving the game.

Opposite of retention.

---

## Session Length

Average time spent per play session.

---

## Sessions per Day

How often players return daily.

---

## Stickiness

Informal term for engagement quality.

Often approximated by:

```
DAU / MAU
```

# Chapter 3: Monetization Models

---

## Monetization

How a game generates revenue.

---

## Premium Model

One-time purchase.

Example:

- Traditional console games

---

## Free-to-Play (F2P)

Game is free, monetized through optional spending.

---

## Hybrid Model

Combination of premium + in-game purchases.

---

## Microtransactions

Small purchases within a game.

---

## Cosmetic Monetization

Selling visual changes only:

- Skins
- Emotes
- Costumes

Important design principle:

> Avoid pay-to-win imbalance.

---

## Pay-to-Win (P2W)

When purchases give gameplay advantage.

Highly controversial.

---

## Battle Pass

Progression-based monetization system.

Structure:

- Earn rewards through gameplay
- Premium track unlocks extra rewards

---

## Gacha System

Randomized reward monetization.

Common in:

- Mobile games
- Some RPG systems

Based on probability mechanics.

---

## Loot Box

Randomized reward container.

Similar to gacha but terminology varies by region.

---

## Whale (Industry Term)

High-spending player.

A small percentage of players generating large revenue share.

---

## ARPU (Average Revenue Per User)

Revenue per player.

---

## ARPPU (Average Revenue Per Paying User)

Revenue per paying user only.

---

## LTV (Lifetime Value)

Total predicted revenue from a player over time.

---

# Chapter 4: Economy Design (Game Systems Layer)

---

## In-Game Economy

System of currency, rewards, and sinks.

---

## Currency Sink

Mechanism that removes currency from the economy.

Examples:

- Upgrades
- Repairs
- Crafting costs

Prevents inflation.

---

## Currency Faucet

Mechanism that introduces currency.

Examples:

- Loot drops
- Rewards
- Quests

---

## Inflation (Game Economy)

When currency value decreases due to oversupply.

---

## Resource Scarcity

Limiting resources to create meaningful decisions.

---

## Reward Loop

System that reinforces engagement through rewards.

---

## Daily Quests

## Weekly Challenges

# Chapter 7: Player Segmentation (Advanced Analytics)

---

## Cohort

Group of players sharing a characteristic.

Example:

- Players who joined in Week 1

---

## Cohort Analysis

Tracking behavior of groups over time.

Used for:

- Retention trends
- Monetization behavior

---

## Segmentation

Dividing players by behavior type.

Examples:

- Casual players
- Competitive players
- Paying users

---

## Behavioral Funnels

Tracking progression through systems.

Example:

```
Install → Tutorial → First Match → First Win → First Purchase
```

# Chapter 8: Monetization Ethics & Criticism

---

## Ethical Monetization

Designing systems that:

- Avoid manipulation
- Respect player agency
- Maintain fairness

---

## Dark Patterns (Negative Term)

Design techniques that exploit psychology unfairly.

Examples:

- Hidden costs
- Forced urgency
- Obscured probabilities

---

## Paywall

Blocking content behind payment.

---

## Content Gating

Restricting access based on progression or payment.

---

---

## KPI (Key Performance Indicator)

Core performance metric.

Examples:

- Retention
- Revenue
- Engagement

---

## A/B Testing

Comparing two versions of a system.

Example:

Different UI layouts tested for conversion.

---

## Conversion Rate

Percentage of players who complete a desired action.

Example:

Purchase conversion rate.

---

## Funnel Drop-Off

Where players stop progressing.

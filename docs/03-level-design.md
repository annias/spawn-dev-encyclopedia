# Chapter 3 — Level Design Encyclopedia

## Thinking Like a Professional Level Designer

One of the biggest misconceptions among new developers is that level design is about **building environments**.

It isn't.

Environment art builds environments.

Level design creates **player experiences through space.**

A professional level designer is fundamentally a behavioral architect.

Their job is not:

> "Create a cool map."

Their job is:

> "Create a sequence of decisions, emotions, challenges, discoveries, and behaviors that occur through interaction with space."

Senior level designers rarely think in terms of walls, doors, corridors, and props.

They think in terms of:

- Information
- Attention
- Movement
- Pressure
- Choice
- Risk
- Reward
- Pacing
- Psychology

The map itself is merely the medium.

---

# The Fundamental Equation of Level Design

Professional level designers often operate from an implicit model:

```
Space+Rules+Information+Player Ability=Player Behavior
```

Everything in level design ultimately serves one purpose:

> Shaping player behavior.

---

# Chapter 1: The Three Layers of a Level

Every level exists simultaneously on three layers.

## Physical Layer

The literal geometry.

Examples:

- Walls
- Platforms
- Buildings
- Terrain
- Cover objects

This is what beginners focus on.

---

## Functional Layer

The gameplay purpose.

Examples:

- Combat space
- Exploration space
- Puzzle space
- Social space

Professionals spend most of their time here.

---

## Experiential Layer

The emotional outcome.

Examples:

- Tension
- Curiosity
- Relief
- Wonder
- Fear

Elite level designers start here.

They ask:

> What should the player feel?

Then build backwards.

---

# Chapter 2: Flow

Flow is one of the most important concepts in level design.

Definition:

> The smooth progression of player attention, movement, and decision-making through a space.

---

## Good Flow

Player naturally understands:

- Where they are
- Where they came from
- Where they're going
- What they're doing

Without needing explicit instruction.

---

## Bad Flow

Player experiences:

- Confusion
- Backtracking
- Frustration
- Getting lost

---

## Common Misconception

Beginners think flow means:

> Moving quickly.

Not true.

A horror game may deliberately slow movement.

Flow means:

> Appropriate movement for intended experience.

---

## Types of Flow

### Linear Flow

One primary route.

Examples:

- Half-Life
- Uncharted

Advantages:

- Tight pacing
- Strong narrative control

---

### Branching Flow

Multiple routes.

Examples:

- Dishonored
- Deus Ex

Advantages:

- Agency
- Replayability

---

### Hub-and-Spoke

Central hub connected to regions.

Examples:

- Dark Souls
- Metroid Prime

Advantages:

- Structured exploration

---

### Open Flow

Player creates their own route.

Examples:

- Minecraft
- Breath of the Wild

Advantages:

- Emergence
- Freedom

Challenge:

- Harder pacing control

---

# Chapter 3: Spatial Design

Spatial design is the discipline of controlling experience through geometry.

---

## Macro Space

Large-scale layout.

Questions:

- How regions connect
- Major pathways
- World structure

Examples:

- Open world regions
- Dungeon structure
- Multiplayer map layout

---

## Meso Space

Intermediate structures.

Examples:

- Buildings
- Encounter zones
- Districts

---

## Micro Space

Moment-to-moment interaction areas.

Examples:

- Cover placements
- Platform jumps
- Loot locations

Many junior designers focus only on micro-space.

Senior designers work across all three scales simultaneously.

---

# Chapter 4: Affordances

One of the most important design concepts in existence.

Affordance:

> What an object suggests can be done with it.

Examples:

|Object|Affordance|
|---|---|
|Ladder|Climb|
|Door|Open|
|Lever|Pull|
|Cover wall|Hide|

---

## Why Affordances Matter

Players rarely read instructions.

They interpret environments.

Good affordances reduce tutorial requirements.

---

## False Affordance

Suggesting interaction that does not exist.

Example:

Detailed door that cannot open.

Players hate this.

---

## Hidden Affordance

Interaction exists but isn't communicated.

Example:

Climbable wall with no visual cues.

Players miss it.

---

# Signifiers

Often confused with affordances.

Affordance:

> Possible action.

Signifier:

> Information communicating affordance.

Example:

Yellow paint in modern games.

The paint isn't the affordance.

The climbable ledge is.

The paint is the signifier.

---

# Chapter 5: Sightlines

Sightlines are critical.

Definition:

> What players can see from a given position.

Professional level designers obsess over sightlines.

---

## Long Sightlines

Allow:

- Reconnaissance
- Sniping
- Anticipation

Common in:

- Battlefield
- ARMA

---

## Short Sightlines

Create:

- Ambushes
- Tension
- Surprise

Common in:

- Horror games
- Close-quarters shooters

---

## Information Sightlines

Used for navigation.

Player sees:

- Landmark
- Objective
- Exit

before reaching it.

This creates orientation.

---

# Chapter 6: Landmarking

Humans navigate using landmarks.

Players do too.

---

## Landmark

A unique environmental feature used for orientation.

Examples:

- Castle
- Tower
- Statue
- Mountain

---

## Strong Landmark Characteristics

Must be:

### Visible

Seen from multiple locations.

### Distinct

Unique silhouette.

### Memorable

Psychologically sticky.

---

## Weak Landmark

Everything looks similar.

Players get lost.

This is a common issue in procedural environments.

---

# Chapter 7: Wayfinding

Wayfinding is:

> Helping players navigate without explicit instructions.

Elite designers guide players invisibly.

---

## Techniques

### Lighting

Bright areas attract attention.

---

### Color

Contrast attracts attention.

---

### Motion

Movement naturally draws the eye.

---

### Framing

Architecture can point toward objectives.

---

### Leading Lines

Roads.

Pipes.

Railings.

Hallways.

All direct vision.

---

### Environmental Storytelling

Players follow clues.

Example:

Blood trail leading to objective.

---

# Golden Path

Professional term.

Definition:

> The route most players are expected to take.

Not the only route.

The preferred route.

Designers often ask:

> "Can players identify the golden path?"

---

# Critical Path

Definition:

> The minimum sequence required to complete objectives.

The golden path often overlaps with the critical path.

Not always.

---

# Chapter 8: Encounter Design

An encounter is:

> A deliberately crafted gameplay situation.

Examples:

- Combat
- Puzzle
- Stealth
- Dialogue

---

# Combat Encounters

Professional encounter design balances:

- Space
- Enemies
- Resources
- Objectives

Not just enemy placement.

---

## Combat Triangle

Many shooters balance:

```
CoverMobilitySightlines
```

Poor balance creates stagnant gameplay.

---

## Arena Design

Arena:

Contained combat space.

Questions:

- Where can players move?
- Where is safety?
- Where is danger?

---

## Combat Beats

Encounter rhythm.

Example:

```
Weak enemies↓Pressure increase↓Elite enemy↓Relief
```

Good encounters create emotional curves.

---

# Chapter 9: Choke Points

Definition:

A narrow area controlling movement.

Examples:

- Doorways
- Bridges
- Tunnels

---

## Uses

Control:

- Movement
- Combat
- Information

---

## Multiplayer Usage

Important for:

- Territory control
- Defensive positions

---

## Risks

Too many choke points:

Creates stagnation.

Players feel trapped.

---

# Chapter 10: Pacing

Pacing may be the most important advanced level design skill.

Definition:

> The control of intensity over time.

---

# Intensity Curve

Great levels rarely maintain constant intensity.

Instead:

```
Calm↓Build↓Peak↓Recovery↓Build↓Peak
```

---

## Compression

Increasing intensity.

---

## Release

Reducing intensity.

---

## Escalation

Introducing new variables.

Examples:

- New enemy type
- Environmental hazard
- Boss

---

# Chapter 11: Metrics

Metrics are measurements used to build levels.

Professionals rely heavily on metrics.

---

## Examples

Character height.

Jump distance.

Sprint speed.

Weapon range.

Cover height.

Door width.

---

## Why Metrics Matter

Without metrics:

Levels become inconsistent.

Consistency creates readability.

---

# Grayboxing / Blockouts

Professional workflow:

```
Gameplay↓Testing↓Iteration↓Art Pass
```

NOT:

```
Art↓Gameplay
```

---

## Graybox

Simple geometry used to test design.

Examples:

- Cubes
- Planes
- Basic shapes

Purpose:

Validate gameplay.

---

# Chapter 12: Player Funnels

A funnel narrows player movement.

Used for:

- Guidance
- Pacing
- Dramatic reveals

---

## Hard Funnel

Physical restriction.

Examples:

- Corridor
- Locked gate

---

## Soft Funnel

Psychological guidance.

Examples:

- Lighting
- Visual interest
- Objectives

Senior designers prefer soft funnels whenever possible.

---

# Chapter 13: Environmental Storytelling

One of the most misunderstood concepts.

Definition:

> Narrative communicated through environment rather than exposition.

---

## Example

Destroyed laboratory.

Broken containment chamber.

Blood trail.

No dialogue needed.

Player constructs story.

---

# Passive Storytelling

Information exists.

Player may ignore it.

---

# Active Storytelling

Player interaction reveals story.

Examples:

- Notes
- Audio logs
- Interactive objects

# Chapter 15: Senior-Level Vocabulary

These are phrases you will hear in professional reviews.

---

## Readability

Can players understand what's happening?

---

## Visual Noise

Information that competes for attention.

---

## Cognitive Load

Mental effort required.

---

## Decision Density

Number of meaningful decisions per minute.

---

## Dead Space

Area lacking meaningful interaction.

---

## Kill Zone

Area exposed to danger.

---

## Safe Space

Area offering recovery.

---

## Combat Readability

Can players interpret threats?

---

## Traversal Language

Consistent communication of movement opportunities.

---

## Environmental Language

Repeated visual rules players learn.

Example:

Yellow = climbable.

Red barrel = explosive.

---

# The Master Mental Model of Level Design

Beginners think:

> "How do I build this environment?"

Intermediate designers think:

> "How do I create this gameplay?"

Senior designers think:

> "How do I shape player behavior?"

Expert designers think:

> "How do I shape player psychology through space, information, and choice?"

That final transition is what separates a map creator from a true level designer.

---

# Professional Exercise

When playing any game, stop every 5 minutes and ask:

1. What behavior is this space encouraging?
2. What behavior is it discouraging?
3. What information is being revealed?
4. What information is being hidden?
5. Where is the player's attention being directed?
6. What emotional state is the designer attempting to create?
7. What level-design techniques are being used to achieve it?

If you can consistently answer those questions, you will begin to see levels the way professional designers do.

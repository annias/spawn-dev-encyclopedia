    GAME DEVELOPMENT LANGUAGE & CONCEPTS ENCYCLOPEDIA

    Chapter 8 — Production & Project Management Terminology

    The Core Mental Shift: What “Production” Means in Game Development

    Production is often misunderstood as “scheduling.”

    In reality, production is:

    The system that converts creative intent into shippable work across time, people, and constraints.

    A senior production mindset is not:

    • “Are we on schedule?”

    It is:

    • “Is the system producing usable output at a sustainable rate?”

    Production is fundamentally about:

    • Flow of work
    • Risk management
    • Dependency resolution
    • Communication structure
    • Scope control

    Chapter 1: The Role of Production in a Studio

    Production exists to solve a core problem:

    Creative work does not naturally organize itself into predictable output.

    Without production:

    • Teams duplicate work
    • Dependencies block progress
    • Scope expands uncontrollably
    • Deadlines fail

    Producer (Core Role)

    A producer is responsible for:

    • Delivery of milestones
    • Coordination across departments
    • Risk tracking
    • Scope management

    They do NOT typically:

    • Design systems
    • Write code
    • Create art

    They manage flow of production, not content itself.

    Project Manager (PM)

    Focus:

    Execution tracking and schedule adherence.

    Differences from Producer:

    • PM = timeline + task tracking
    • Producer = creative + production alignment

    Development Director

    High-level leadership role overseeing:

    • Production strategy
    • Cross-team alignment
    • Long-term planning

    Product Owner

    More common in agile environments.

    Focus:

    • Defining feature priority
    • Representing product vision
    • Managing backlog value

    Chapter 2: Production Lifecycle Language

    Milestone

    A major checkpoint in development.

    Examples:

    • Prototype complete
    • Vertical slice complete
    • Alpha
    • Beta

    Milestones are used for:

    • Publisher reviews
    • Funding releases
    • Internal validation

    Gate

    A decision point where progress is approved or rejected.

    Example:

    “We will not proceed to Beta until performance targets are met.”

    Deliverable

    A tangible output.

    Examples:

    • Playable build
    • Art asset pack
    • Design document

    Dependency

    A task that must be completed before another can start.

    Example:

    • Animation system required before combat tuning

    Blocker

    Something preventing progress entirely.

    Example:

    • Critical bug preventing builds from running

    Risk

    Anything that could threaten delivery.

    Types:

    • Technical risk
    • Scope risk
    • Schedule risk
    • Creative risk

    Chapter 3: Agile Development Vocabulary

    Agile Development

    Iterative development methodology focused on:

    • Flexibility
    • Incremental progress
    • Continuous feedback

    Scrum

    A structured agile framework.

    Key elements:

    • Sprints
    • Backlog
    • Stand-ups

    Sprint

    A fixed time period (usually 1–3 weeks) where work is completed.

    Backlog

    A prioritized list of work items.

    Includes:

    • Features
    • Bugs
    • Tasks

    Velocity

    Amount of work a team completes per sprint.

    Used for forecasting.

    Burndown Chart

    Visual tracking of remaining work over time.

    Stand-up

    Daily team meeting.

    Focus:

    • What was done
    • What will be done
    • What is blocked

    Chapter 4: Scope Management (Critical AAA Concept)

    Scope

    Total amount of work required to complete a project.

    Scope Creep

    Uncontrolled expansion of scope.

    Example:

    • Adding features without adjusting time or budget

    Feature Creep

    Adding unnecessary features.

    Often causes:

    • Delays
    • Complexity explosion

    Scope Lock

    Point where new features are no longer added.

    Used to stabilize production.

    MVP (Minimum Viable Product)

    Smallest playable version of a game.

    Used for:

    • Testing core fun
    • Validating design

    Vertical Slice vs MVP

    Concept Purpose
    MVP Test core mechanics
    Vertical Slice Show final-quality vision

    Chapter 5: Scheduling Language

    Milestone Planning

    Structuring development into phases.

    Roadmap

    High-level timeline of major deliverables.

    Pipeline

    Flow of work from concept to completion.

    Bottleneck

    Stage slowing down entire pipeline.

    Example:

    • Animation team overloaded blocking gameplay integration

    Critical Path

    Sequence of tasks that determines project duration.

    Chapter 6: Resource Management

    Resource Allocation

    Assigning people to tasks.

    Example:

    • 3 artists on environment
    • 2 engineers on gameplay

    Headcount

    Number of people on a team.

    Bandwidth

    Available capacity of a team member.

    Example:

    • “Artist is at 80% bandwidth”

    Burn Rate

    How quickly money is being spent.

    Critical in studios.

    Chapter 7: Communication Systems in Production

    Sync

    Cross-team alignment meeting.

    Cross-Discipline Review

    Meeting where design, art, and engineering align.

    Feedback Loop (Production Context)

    Cycle of:

    • Build → Review → Adjust → Repeat

    Approval Chain

    Sequence of sign-offs required before changes are accepted.

    Example:

    Designer → Lead → Director → Producer

    Documentation

    Written communication of systems.

    Includes:

    • Design docs (GDD)
    • Technical specs
    • Art briefs

    Chapter 8: Design Documentation Language

    GDD (Game Design Document)

    Living document describing game systems.

    Modern GDDs are often:

    • Modular
    • Iterative
    • Lightweight

    Not massive static PDFs.

    TDD (Technical Design Document)

    Describes system architecture.

    Used by engineers.

    Art Brief

    Instructions for asset creation.

    Includes:

    • Style
    • Reference
    • Constraints
    • Function

    One-Pager

    Short design pitch document.

    Used for early concepts.

    Chapter 9: Risk Management Language

    Risk Register

    List of known risks and mitigation strategies.

    Mitigation

    Steps taken to reduce risk.

    Contingency Plan

    Backup plan if something fails.

    Technical Debt

    Shortcuts in code or systems that must be fixed later.

    Common phrase:

    “We’ll pay that debt later.”

    But often becomes permanent.

    Chapter 10: Production Reality in AAA vs Indie

    AAA Production

    Characteristics:

    • Heavy structure
    • Large teams
    • Formal approval chains
    • High documentation overhead

    Indie Production

    Characteristics:

    • Flexible roles
    • Rapid iteration
    • Minimal bureaucracy
    • Faster decision cycles

    Live Service Production

    Characteristics:

    • Continuous delivery
    • Seasonal content cycles
    • Analytics-driven decisions
    • Constant updates

    Chapter 11: Studio Communication Language

    “On Track”

    Means:

    • Current schedule is achievable

    “At Risk”

    Means:

    • Likely delay unless mitigated

    “Blocked”

    Means:

    • Work cannot proceed

    “Green / Yellow / Red Status”

    Color-coded project health system:

    • Green = healthy
    • Yellow = caution
    • Red = critical issue

    Chapter 12: Senior Production Mental Model

    Beginner Thinking

    “Are we finishing tasks?”

    Intermediate Thinking

    “Are we meeting deadlines?”

    Senior Thinking

    “Is the system of work sustainable, predictable, and aligned with creative goals?”

    Expert Thinking

    “How do we structure human effort so that unpredictable creative output becomes reliably shippable software under real-world constraints?”

    Key Insight

    In professional studios:

    Production is not bureaucracy — it is the nervous system of game development.

    Without it:

    • Creativity collapses into chaos
    • Or rigidity collapses into stagnation

    Good production enables both creativity and delivery simultaneously.
